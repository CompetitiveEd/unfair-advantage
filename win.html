THE FRAMEWORK

STEP 0 OF 9

The Pre-Mortem

MANDATORY
Stop Optimism Bias. Before you write a word, predict exactly how you will lose. If you can't name the reason, you haven't found the risk.
G-15 // DEAL PRE-MORTEM SIMULATOR
It is 6 months in the future. We have lost the [CLIENT] deal. The loss review states three reasons: 1. The Political Failure (We backed the wrong person). 2. The Value Failure (We were cheaper but "too risky"). 3. The "Gotcha" (We missed a hidden requirement). Based on the [RFP DOCUMENTS] and [STAKEHOLDER MAP], generate the specific narrative for each of these three failures. Then, for each failure, give me the ONE action I must take today to prevent it.
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Political Failure: "We relied on the CIO (Richard Hale) but the COO (Harrison) blocked us because our transition plan disrupted his operation."
Prevention: Create a 'Zero-Disruption Transition Plan' specifically for Harrison, presented before submission.

01
STEP 1 OF 9

Win Themes

~20 MIN
Win themes are not slogans. They're the three strategic threads that run through every volume of your response.
W3-01 // WIN THEME GENERATOR
Act as a Proposal Strategist. Context: We are bidding for [CLIENT] to provide [SERVICE]. Our Deal Strategy (W2-07) identified these priorities: [PASTE DEAL STRATEGY]. Task: Develop 3 'Gold-Standard Win Themes'. Criteria for a Win Theme: 1. It must solve a specific client pain point. 2. It must be provable with evidence we have. 3. It must be hard for competitors to claim. [PASTE CONTEXT/INPUTS HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Theme 1: "Transformation with Discipline." (Combines Innovation with Harrison's need for safety).
Theme 2: "Resilience as Operational DNA." (Aligns with Jennifer Morris's PRA focus).
Theme 3: "Commercial Alignment." (Uses the Gain-Share model to prove we stand behind the outcome).

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Theme deployment is what separates good bids from great ones. A win theme that only appears in the executive summary is a slogan. A win theme that appears in the technical response, the commercial proposal, the oral presentation, AND the informal stakeholder conversations is a strategy.

02
STEP 2 OF 9

Competitor Intelligence

~15 MIN
You're not bidding in a vacuum. The evaluation panel is comparing you against specific alternatives. This prompt builds the competitive landscape.
W3-02 // COMPETITOR INTELLIGENCE SYNTHESIS
Act as a Competitive Intelligence Analyst. We are competing against [COMPETITOR A] and [COMPETITOR B]. Create a 'Competitive Positioning Matrix' (Markdown Table): 1. Their Likely Pitch: What will they claim is their USP? 2. Their Likely Ghosting: What will they whisper about us? 3. Our Counter-Narrative: How do we inoculate the client against their claims? 4. The 'Kill Shot': Where are they demonstrably weak where we are strong? [PASTE COMPETITOR NAMES HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE (HELIOS vs GIANTS)

Competitor: 'Goliath Corp' (Global Outsourcer).
Their Pitch: "Scale and low cost."
Our Ghosting: "You will be Client #840. You get the C-Team."
Kill Shot: "Helios guarantees UK-only delivery. Goliath will offshore your regulated data."

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The 'if they say, we say' responses are the most practical output. Knowing what they'll claim lets you pre-position. Not by attacking them — by making your differentiation obvious without naming anyone.

03
STEP 3 OF 9

Scoring Strategy

~20 MIN
Not every requirement deserves equal effort. A criterion worth 25% of the total score deserves five times the writing investment.
W3-03 // TIER-BY-TIER SCORING STRATEGY
Act as a Bid Manager optimizing for maximum weighted score. I am providing the Evaluation Criteria and Weightings. Create a 'Scoring Strategy Table': 1. Rank criteria by 'Weighted Value'. 2. Define exactly what evidence/case studies we must include to score a 5/5. 3. Define the 'Minimum Viable Response' for low-value questions. [PASTE CRITERIA AND WEIGHTINGS HERE]
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Junior bid managers try to get 5/5 on everything. Senior bid managers know that 60% of the RFP is "Pass/Fail" hygiene. They spend 80% of their energy on the 20% of questions that are actually weighted for differentiation (Innovation, Cultural Fit, Transition Risk).

04
STEP 4 OF 9

Executive Summary

~30 MIN
The executive summary is the most important section. Most are about the bidder. This one is about the client.
W3-04 // EXECUTIVE SUMMARY GENERATOR
Act as a professional copywriter using the 'Made to Stick' framework. Draft the structure of the Executive Summary. Structure it in 4 Acts: Act 1: The Context (The 'Why Now'). Act 2: The Complication (The 'Villain'). Act 3: The Resolution (The 'Hero'). NOT US. The Hero is the 'New Way of Working'. Act 4: The Proof (Why Us). Embed our 3 Win Themes (W3-01) throughout. [PASTE WIN THEMES AND CONTEXT HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

The Villain: "The availability gap." (Legacy infra + rising expectations).
The Hero: "Meridian 2030." (Their own vision).
The Proof: "Helios has already delivered this journey for Excalibur Financial. We are 9 months ahead of where you need to be."

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The accusation audit embedded naturally is the subtle art. Don't open with 'We know you've been burned.' Instead: 'Investments that delivered below expectation are a pattern we've seen.' You've acknowledged the elephant. You've normalised it.

05
STEP 5 OF 9

Self-Score

~20 MIN
Score your own response before they do. This prompt acts as an independent evaluator.
W3-05 // PRE-SUBMISSION SELF-SCORING
Act as a hostile procurement evaluator. I am providing you with the Criteria and our Draft Response. Task: 1. Score our response out of 5 based STRICTLY on the criteria. Be harsh. 2. Identify: "Where did we answer the question but fail to provide evidence?" 3. Provide 3 specific bullet points on how to move the score up by +1 point. [PASTE CRITERIA AND DRAFT RESPONSE HERE]
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The "Self-Scoring Hubris" failure is real. Internal teams read what they *meant* to write. An AI (or an external reviewer) reads what is actually on the page. Use this prompt to find the "Evidence Gap."

06
STEP 6 OF 9

Orals Strategy

~30 MIN
The opening 90 seconds set the tone. Match speaker credibility to stakeholder roles.
W3-06 // ORAL PRESENTATION STRATEGY
Act as a Presentation Coach for a high-stakes pitch. Context: We are presenting to [CLIENT]. Task: 1. Script the 'First 90 Seconds': A hook that grabs attention. NOT "Hello, we are Helios." 2. Speaker Allocation Strategy: Match speaker credibility to stakeholder roles. 3. The 'Q&A Prep': Based on Risks (W2-07), what are the 3 hardest questions they will ask? [PASTE CONTEXT/RISKS HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Speaker Strategy: Claire Beaumont (MD) opens to match Richard Hale (CIO) — peer to peer. Rachel Okonkwo (Resilience) answers all Jennifer Morris's questions. Do NOT let the Sales Director answer technical questions.
The Hook: "We aren't here to sell you IT. We're here to guarantee the £3.1BN of premium that flows through it."

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Sandhurst "Mission Command" applies here: Establish you understand their mission better than they have articulated it themselves. Once they feel understood, they stop evaluating and start listening.

07
STEP 7 OF 9

Engagement Storyboard

~20 MIN
The storyboard turns your deal strategy into a visual timeline. Every touchpoint planned.
W3-07 // ENGAGEMENT STORYBOARD
Act as a Pursuit Manager. Create a 'Pursuit Storyboard' (Timeline from now until Decision Date). Output a Mermaid.js gantt chart mapping: 1. Submission Deadlines (Backward pass). 2. Key Governance Gates. 3. Stakeholder Engagement Points. 4. Clarification Question Strategy. [PASTE TIMELINE/DEADLINES HERE]
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Most teams plan the document. Winning teams plan the *influence*. The storyboard forces you to plan when you will speak to the CFO, not just when you will write the CFO's section.

08
STEP 8 OF 9

Response Writer

~45 MIN
Drafting specific response sections. Opens with their challenge, not your capability. States your approach specifically, not generically.
W3-08 // RESPONSE SECTION WRITER
Act as a senior bid writer producing a response section. Inputs: 1. The specific requirement/question. 2. Evaluation criterion. 3. The win theme to embed. 4. Primary evaluator profile. 5. Proof points to include. 6. Word limit. Write a response that: - Opens with THEIR challenge, not your capability. - States your approach specifically. - Embeds the win theme naturally. - Provides evidence for every claim. [PASTE INPUTS HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE (THE HARD QUESTION)

Question: "Confirm you hold ISO 22301 certification. If not, justify why we should trust your resilience."
Helios Answer: We do not hold it today. We are targeting Q4 2026. However, our methodology has already delivered certification for [Client A] and [Client B]. We are not asking you to trust our certificate; we are asking you to trust the team that built the framework you are now adopting.

09
STEP 9 OF 9

Commercial Narrative

~30 MIN
The strategic framing that shapes how evaluators interpret the numbers. Price vs Value. Investment vs Cost.
W3-09 // COMMERCIAL NARRATIVE GENERATOR
Act as a commercial strategist. Write the narrative wrapper around our pricing (NOT the schedule itself). Produce: 1. COMMERCIAL PHILOSOPHY: How our model aligns with their stated goals. 2. VALUE FRAMING: Price vs Cost of Inaction / TCO. 3. DIFFERENTIATORS: Gain-share, innovation funds, outcome links. 4. INVESTMENT NARRATIVE: Frame transition costs as investment with payback timeline. 5. OBJECTION PRE-EMPTION: "Why higher than X?" [PASTE PRICING MODEL/STRATEGY HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Framing: "Year 1 is an investment year. You are paying for the transformation that lowers Year 3-7 costs by 20%. We share the risk of this transition by deferring 50% of transformation fees until quality milestones are met."

WHERE THIS BREAKS

FAILURE 1: Win Themes That Weren't Provable

Evaluators loved the executive summary. Then they read the technical response and couldn't find the evidence.

RULE: If you can't point to a specific case study — it's not a win theme.

FAILURE 2: Self-Scoring Hubris

Gave ourselves 4.5/5. Actual evaluation came back at 3.2. The internal team knew what they MEANT, not what they WROTE.

RULE: Ask someone who didn't write it to score it.

FAILURE 3: The Polished Presentation That Missed

Spent two days rehearsing the orals. Slides were immaculate. Delivery was smooth. The panel asked one question: "How would you handle the TUPE transfer for the Manchester team?" Our best speaker — the sales director — froze. The technical lead who knew the answer wasn't presenting.

RULE: The person who answers the hardest question must be in the room and briefed to lead.
