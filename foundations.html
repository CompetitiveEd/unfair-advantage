The Commercial Operating System: Why Judgement is the Moat in an AI-Driven World.

The 2026 Market Pattern

The hardest roles to fill in 2026 are those blending technical literacy with commercial acumen and interpersonal skills. The differentiator isn’t just AI fluency—it’s the human judgement that knows what to do with what AI finds.

EQ + IQ + AI = Sales Performance

AI handles the scanning; you handle the pattern recognition, the political dynamics, and the timing.

The Story: How Research Won a Major Deal

Tom, a veteran AE, lost a deal because the competitor "seemed to understand our business better". They had used the exact language the executive team was using internally.

For his next pitch, Tom used AI to scan annual reports and earnings calls. He found "cost-to-serve" mentioned 17 times, while "innovation" appeared only twice.

Outcome: Tom repositioned from "digital transformation" to "operational efficiency with managed risk" and won a multi-year contract.

The IQ & EQ Frameworks

Timme & Astley — Insight-Led Selling

Adopt an executive mindset: Shift from "what am I selling?" to "what are THEY trying to solve?". Use THEIR language—not vendor jargon.

Chris Voss — Tactical Empathy

The "Accusation Audit": Surfacing their objections or past project failures before they voice them. Prove you understand their world before asking for a commitment.

McKinsey — Second-Order Thinking

Move past the surface. If they want "innovation," second-order thinking asks: "Have they been burned before? Do they actually want efficiency without the risk?".

The Hierarchy of Executive Needs

Most sellers stop at the department level. Executives think in a hierarchy:

  • 1. Goals: Vision, financial targets, board imperatives.
  • 2. Strategies: Key pillars and competitive positioning.
  • 3. Initiatives: Specific transformation programmes and projects.
  • 4. Departments: Daily implementation and operational activity.

Honesty Check: When AI Gets It Wrong

Maintaining executive credibility requires knowing the failure modes:

  • Hallucinations: If AI can't cite a page number or quote, it doesn't exist.
  • Context Collapse: AI may cite an executive who left 18 months ago; always verify with LinkedIn.
  • The Over-Reliance Trap: Generic AI questions yield generic "everyone asks that" responses.
