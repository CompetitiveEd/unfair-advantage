Case 03: The Founder Trap

Boutique technology firm. £9M revenue. One person winning every deal. Sellers maintaining accounts, not growing them. No accountability culture. No stakeholder mapping. Financial reporting unusable for decision-making. The business was the founder. The founder was the business.

The Diagnosis

Three fractures. Sellers without licence to act — waiting for the founder to tell them what to do. No capability infrastructure — no account intelligence, no whitespace mapping, no stakeholder planning. Motivation driven by fear of the founder, not shared purpose.

The Fix

Not a sales training programme. Structural. New sales leadership to free the founder from daily ops. Restructured finance for decision-quality data. Deliberate pivot to the market where customer intimacy ran deepest.

The Result

The founder went from doing everything to doing the one thing that matters: making judgment calls with time to think. Sales team doubled. Quarterly customer events became an industry fixture — 75 out of 76 invited attendees at the last one. AI and expert panels now part of the programme. The founder is on a 3-year exit. The business runs without him.

A founder-dependent £9M business is worth less than a self-sustaining one. The multiple changes even if revenue doesn't.

Where This Breaks (Failure Modes)

The Middle Management Gap

The GAIN methodology relies on aligning with C-Suite financial mandates. If your team is too terrified to bypass the VP of IT or the Procurement Lead, the strategy will fail. Middle managers buy features; boards buy risk mitigation.

The Confidence Problem

You can give a team the perfect AI-generated executive dossier. But if they lack the presence and behavioural confidence to walk into a boardroom and challenge a CFO's worldview, the data is useless. Tools do not equal talent.
