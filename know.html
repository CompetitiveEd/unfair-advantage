20 MINUTES — FROM ANNUAL REPORT TO EXECUTIVE BRIEFING
THE PSYCHOLOGY: DRIVER 01 (SAFETY)

Research is not about gathering facts. It is about lowering buyer cortisol. Humans are biologically wired to fear vendor failure. When you ask basic discovery questions ("Tell me about your priorities"), you trigger that fear. When you quote their own earnings call back to them to frame your solution, you prove you are a safe pair of hands. This core prompt chain ensures you never walk into a room blind.

[G-02] Public Data

9-Pillar Deep Research

[+ VIEW]
Extracts the intelligence architecture of the company. Returns board-level priorities, budget signals, and pain points structured for sales.
Act as a Senior Market Intelligence Analyst preparing a pre-meeting briefing for an enterprise sales executive.

Using the source data provided, extract intelligence across these 9 pillars:
1. STRATEGIC PRIORITIES: Board-level goals and CEO stated direction. Quote exact language.
2. FINANCIAL SIGNALS: Revenue trends, margin pressure, investment areas. Flag numbers.
3. PAIN POINTS: Stated challenges, risk disclosures, failed initiatives.
4. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING: How they describe their market position. Who do they benchmark against?
5. TECHNOLOGY DIRECTION: Digital transformation priorities, platform investments.
6. REGULATORY & COMPLIANCE: Upcoming deadlines, certification targets, audit findings.
7. PEOPLE & CULTURE: Hiring priorities, skills gaps, leadership changes.
8. STAKEHOLDER LANGUAGE: The specific phrases executives use repeatedly.
9. TIMELINE & DEADLINES: Fiscal year, budget cycles, programme milestones.

FORMAT: Structure each pillar with 3-5 key findings. For every claim, cite the source document. Flag anything you cannot verify as "UNVERIFIED."
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Pay attention to Pillar 8 (Stakeholder Language). If the CEO says "Continuous Business Availability" and you pitch "Disaster Recovery," you sound like a generic vendor. If you use their exact language, you sound like an insider.

[G-04] Public Data

Budget Signal Detection

[+ VIEW]
Most sellers pitch "invest more." Professionals find money the prospect is already wasting. This prompt finds the reallocation budget.
Using the company research, identify "Boring But Expensive" problems — operational costs that are high, visible to the CFO, and solvable but unglamorous.

Look for these categories:
1. MANUAL PROCESSES: Workflows requiring human intervention that could be automated.
2. INTEGRATION FRICTION: Data reconciliation between systems, manual handoffs.
3. COMPLIANCE BURDEN: Audit preparation labour, certification maintenance.
4. VENDOR DEPENDENCY: Over-reliance creating premium pricing or switching costs.
5. TECHNICAL DEBT: End-of-life systems, extended support contracts.

For each finding:
— Estimate the pain/cost where data supports it.
— Identify who owns this budget (which function).
— Frame the reallocation argument: "You're currently spending time/money on [boring problem]. Redirecting even a portion toward [solution] eliminates the root cause."
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

If a company mentions a shift from "self-assessment" to "structured vendor interviews" in their risk disclosures, that implies massive manual labour cost. That is your entry point. Solve the boring problem first.

Cross-Source Narrative Gap

[+ VIEW]
Finds the friction between what marketing says on the website and what finance reports to regulators.
I've provided intelligence from multiple sources. Compare them and find the gaps.

1. NARRATIVE CONSISTENCY: Where do the website, LinkedIn, and individual posts tell the same story?
2. NARRATIVE GAPS: Where does the website say one thing and LinkedIn say another? Where do individuals contradict the corporate message?
3. HIDDEN INTELLIGENCE: What did you find on one source that doesn't appear anywhere else? A job posting that reveals a capability gap. An event that signals strategic direction.

FORMAT: For each finding, explain why the gap or signal matters for a sales engagement.

Executive Strategic Intelligence

[+ VIEW]
Builds a profile beyond job titles. Surfaces decision-making style and the "One Question" that proves you understand their agenda.
Analyse the LinkedIn profile, recent posts, and public commentary provided for [NAME], [TITLE].

Build a profile covering:
1. ROLE & RESPONSIBILITIES: What do they actually own? Who do they report to?
2. STATED PRIORITIES: What themes dominate their public commentary?
3. DECISION-MAKING STYLE: Evidence-based or intuitive? Collaborative or directive? Cite specific examples.
4. PROFESSIONAL REPUTATION: How do colleagues describe them in endorsements?
5. PERSONAL DIMENSIONS: Interests outside work mentioned in profile.
6. THE ONE QUESTION: Give me ONE discovery question to ask them that demonstrates I understand their personal agenda. It must make them think: "This person has done their homework."
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

If an executive frequently shares their team's failures publicly, they value transparency over polish. Do not walk into that meeting with a glossy slide deck. Lead with what your solution struggles with.

[G-10] Public Data

Belief System Decoder

[+ VIEW]
Goes beyond "priorities" to map how an executive actually processes truth and risk.
Using the stakeholder profile AND their raw LinkedIn posts/articles — go deeper.
I don't want to know what this person does. I want to know how they decide.

Analyse:
1. CORE CONVICTION: What does this person fundamentally believe? What principle do they return to repeatedly?
2. DECISION TRIGGERS: What makes them say yes? What makes them resist? 
3. COMMUNICATION PATTERNS: Do they use data or narrative? Are they direct or consensus-seeking?
4. TRUST ARCHITECTURE: What earns trust with this person? Speed? Evidence? Honesty about limitations?

FORMAT: Cite the specific source (LinkedIn post date) for every claim.
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

If they frame disagreement as "I don't have sufficient evidence" rather than "I disagree," you know to bring whitepapers, not marketing collateral.

HUMAN CHECKPOINT (EQ OVERRIDE)

Do not run the Synthesis prompts yet. Stop and read the AI's analysis. Does the tone feel right for this specific executive? Are the budget arguments realistic for this sector? The AI has assembled the data—you must now inject your commercial intuition. Filter out the noise.

03. Synthesis & Deliverables
[G-12] Public Data

Synthesise Battle Card

[+ VIEW]
Turns intelligence into ammunition. A single-page reference for the meeting: Hooks, Landmines, and the "North Star".
Using all the intelligence gathered so far — company research, budget signals, and stakeholder profile — create a 1-page Executive Battle Card.

Structure:
1. CFO NORTH STAR: The single strategic priority that defines this company's direction right now. Use their exact language.
2. THE THREE HOOKS: Three conversation-opening insights derived from the research. Each hook must:
   — Reference a specific finding from the research (with source)
   — Connect to a capability you could provide
   — Be framed as an observation, not a pitch ("I noticed..." not "We offer...")
3. ACCUSATION AUDIT: Write 3 statements acknowledging likely concerns. ("You're probably thinking...")
4. BOARDROOM QUESTION: One question designed for a board-level audience that demonstrates strategic understanding.
5. PILLAR 9 INTEGRATION: How does your potential solution connect to their stated strategic direction?
6. LANDMINES: What topics should you AVOID? What past failures should you navigate carefully?
WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The Landmine section is critical. If the prospect recently fired a vendor for a botched cloud migration, talking about your own cloud capabilities too early will trigger their defense mechanisms. You must navigate around the blast radius.

Capability Matching

[+ VIEW]
Don't pitch generic capabilities. Match your proof points to their specific pain points. Requires pasting internal company materials. Use Enterprise AI only.
Based on the company research, stakeholder profile, and battle card I've provided, search the following materials from MY company for the best matches:

[PASTE: Your company's case studies, solution descriptions, or capability statements]

Find:
1. CASE STUDIES: Which client stories are most relevant to this prospect's specific situation? Match on industry, challenge type, scale, and outcomes.
2. PROOF POINTS: Specific metrics or outcomes from our work that directly address their stated pain points.
3. LANGUAGE ALIGNMENT: Where do our materials already use their language? Where do we need to translate?
4. GAPS: Where does this prospect need something we don't have a clear proof point for? These become discovery questions, not weaknesses to hide.
[G-14] Public Data

The Executive Briefing

[+ VIEW]
The final output. Scannable, actionable, and ready to read on a phone 10 minutes before the meeting.
Using all the human-verified intelligence from the previous steps, compile a final Executive Briefing for my meeting with [NAME].

Structure the briefing as follows:

SECTION 1 — STAKEHOLDER PROFILE: Name, title, reporting line. Decision-making style.
SECTION 2 — COMPANY CONTEXT: Strategic direction in 3 sentences using their language. Hard deadlines.
SECTION 3 — HOW WE GOT HERE: The pathway to this meeting. What's the relationship chain?
SECTION 4 — MEETING OBJECTIVES: Primary objective. Secondary fallback. Top 3 discovery questions.
SECTION 5 — PERSONAL DIMENSIONS: Interests, rapport opportunities.
SECTION 6 — KEY MESSAGING: The 3 hooks from the battle card. Accusation audit. What to AVOID.
SECTION 7 — PROOF POINTS: Relevant case studies mapped to their priorities.

This briefing should be ready to review on a phone screen 10 minutes before walking into the room. Concise. Actionable. No filler.

Pre-Mortem Simulator

[+ VIEW]
Dismantles the deal before it fails so you can preemptively secure it.
Based on the company research and battle card:
Imagine it is 6 months from now and we LOST this deal.
Generate the 5 most likely reasons why we lost. (e.g., Status Quo, Price, Stalled, Blocked by Detractor).
For each reason, give me one pre-emptive action to take NOW to prevent it.
[G-17] Public Data

Objection Prep (Accusation Audit)

[+ VIEW]
Prepares the Chris Voss 'Accusation Audit' to disarm negativity before it's spoken.
Based on the company research, stakeholder profile, and battle card — identify the 3 most likely objections this prospect will raise.
For each objection:
1. THE OBJECTION: Be specific. (e.g., "They'll question why our managed service costs more than 4 in-house FTEs.")
2. THE ACCUSATION AUDIT: Write the statement you deploy BEFORE they raise it. Format: "You're probably thinking [specific concern]. Here's what I'd want to know..."
3. THE EVIDENCE: What proof point neutralises this?
4. THE PIVOT: How do you turn the objection into a strength?
5. THE STAKEHOLDER LINK: Which specific stakeholder is most likely to raise this?
[G-18] Public Data

Hallucination Check

[+ VIEW]
Forces the AI to prove its own work to prevent fatal inaccuracies in your briefing.
I am providing the Executive Briefing you generated, and the original source text.
Verify every specific claim in the briefing against the source text.
Output a table:
1. CLAIM: The specific fact/number.
2. SOURCE: Quote the sentence in the source text that proves it.
3. VERDICT: VERIFIED / AMBIGUOUS / HALLUCINATED.
If you cannot find a direct quote, mark it HALLUCINATED.
