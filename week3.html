THE FRAMEWORK
01
STEP 1 OF 7

Win Themes

~20 MIN

Win themes are not slogans. They're the three strategic threads that run through every volume of your response. Each theme must directly address what the scoring structure rewards, be provable with evidence, and be something competitors can't credibly claim.

W3-01 // WIN THEME GENERATOR
Act as a Proposal Strategist.

Context: We are bidding for [CLIENT] to provide [SERVICE].
Our Deal Strategy (W2-07) identified these priorities: [PASTE DEAL STRATEGY].

Task: Develop 3 'Gold-Standard Win Themes'.
Criteria for a Win Theme:
1. It must solve a specific client pain point.
2. It must be provable with evidence we have.
3. It must be hard for competitors to claim.

For each theme, provide:
- The Headline (Short & Punchy).
- The 'So What?' (Why the client cares).
- The Evidence (What proof point supports it?).
- Deployment Guide: How do we weave this into the Executive Summary, the Technical Response, and the Commercial Response?

STEP 2 OF 7

Competitor Intelligence

~15 MIN

You're not bidding in a vacuum. The evaluation panel is comparing you against specific alternatives. This prompt builds the competitive landscape so your win themes are positioned AGAINST something, not just FOR something.

W3-02 // COMPETITOR INTELLIGENCE SYNTHESIS
Act as a Competitive Intelligence Analyst.

We are competing against [COMPETITOR A] and [COMPETITOR B] for this deal.

Create a 'Competitive Positioning Matrix':
1. Their Likely Pitch: What will they claim is their USP?
2. Their Likely Ghosting: What will they whisper about us to the client? (e.g., "Meridian is too expensive," "Meridian is legacy").
3. Our Counter-Narrative: How do we inoculate the client against their claims without naming them directly?
4. The 'Kill Shot': Where are they demonstrably weak where we are demonstrably strong?

03
STEP 3 OF 7

Scoring Strategy

~20 MIN

Not every requirement deserves equal effort. A criterion worth 25% of the total score deserves five times the writing investment of a criterion worth 5%. This prompt maps every criterion to a target score.

W3-03 // TIER-BY-TIER SCORING STRATEGY
Act as a Bid Manager optimizing for maximum weighted score.

I am providing the Evaluation Criteria and Weightings.

Create a 'Scoring Strategy Table':
1. Rank criteria by 'Weighted Value' (Points available).
2. For the Top 3 High-Value Criteria: Define exactly what evidence/case studies we must include to score a 5/5.
3. For Low-Value/Pass-Fail Criteria: Define the 'Minimum Viable Response' to pass without wasting resources.
4. Identify the 'Subjective' Criteria: Which criteria are open to interpretation, and which stakeholder (from W2-05) will likely judge them?

04
STEP 4 OF 7

Executive Summary

~30 MIN

The executive summary is the most important section of any bid response. It's the first thing evaluators read. Most executive summaries are about the bidder. This one is about the client.

W3-04 // EXECUTIVE SUMMARY GENERATOR
Act as a professional copywriter using the 'Made to Stick' framework.

Draft the structure of the Executive Summary for this proposal.

Structure it in 4 Acts:
Act 1: The Context (The 'Why Now'). Acknowledge their situation using their language.
Act 2: The Complication (The 'Villain'). What stops them achieving their goal?
Act 3: The Resolution (The 'Hero'). NOT US. The Hero is the 'New Way of Working'.
Act 4: The Proof (Why Us). The specific evidence that we can deliver the Hero outcome.

Embed our 3 Win Themes (W3-01) throughout.
Tone: Professional, confident, partner-centric. NOT salesy.

W3-05 // PRE-SUBMISSION SELF-SCORING
Act as a hostile procurement evaluator.

I am providing you with:
1. The Evaluation Criteria for Question [X].

2. Identify: "Where did we answer the question but fail to provide evidence?"
3. Identify: "Where did we answer a different question to the one asked?"
4. Provide 3 specific bullet points on how to move the score up by +1 point.

WHERE THIS BREAKS

FAILURE 1: Win Themes That Weren't Provable

Evaluators loved the executive summary. Then they read the technical response and couldn't find the evidence. The themes were aspirational, not provable.

RULE: If you can't point to a specific case study â€” it's not a win theme.

FAILURE 2: Self-Scoring Hubris

Gave ourselves 4.5/5. Actual evaluation came back at 3.2. The internal team knew what they MEANT, not what they WROTE.

RULE: Ask someone who didn't write it to score it.

FAILURE 3: The 90-Second Collapse

The opening slide was a company timeline showing 40 years of heritage. The evaluators' eyes glazed over.

RULE: Your opening must be about THEM, not you.
