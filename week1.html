WEEK 1 — [G] GATHER / KNOW THEM — INTELLIGENCE LAYER

AI handles the storm. You handle the human.

Reclaim 4 hours per meeting. Walk in understanding their board-level priorities before they say a word.

20 MINUTES — FROM ANNUAL REPORT TO EXECUTIVE BRIEFING
THE CONTEXT Tom had 30 minutes before a CFO meeting. He ran the annual report through an AI research prompt. He found the CFO used "cost-to-serve" 17 times and "innovation" twice.

He dropped "digital transformation" from his pitch and led with "operational efficiency." The CFO leaned forward and said: "You're the only one who seemed to understand what we're actually trying to do."
Tom didn't become smarter in 30 minutes. He used AI to surface what experienced sellers already know to look for — then applied judgment to turn research into relevance.
BEFORE YOU BEGIN

GATHER

01: Annual report (PDF from investor relations)
02: Executive LinkedIn posts (last 6 months)
03: Press releases (most recent 3-5)
04: Earnings call transcript (if public)

PREPARE

05: AI synthesis (review output before using)
06: Discovery questions (in THEIR language)
07: Your company materials (for Step 5)
08: Human verification (cross-check key facts)

! No Annual Report? For private companies, use Companies House filings, trade press coverage, and their own website. The prompts adapt, but they need source material.

THE FRAMEWORK

The Prompt Chain

COMPANY INTEL → FIND MONEY → PROFILE PEOPLE → BATTLE CARD → CAPABILITY → BRIEFING

Steps 1-3 extract intelligence and decode decision drivers. Steps 4-5 synthesise strategy. Step 6 creates the deliverable.

01
STEP 1 OF 6

Company Intelligence

~20 MIN
Extracts the intelligence architecture of any company. Returns board-level priorities, budget signals, and pain points structured for sales.
W1-01 // 9-PILLAR DEEP RESEARCH
Act as a Senior Market Intelligence Analyst preparing a pre-meeting briefing for an enterprise sales executive. I will provide: an annual report/shareholder letter, recent press releases, and any available investor relations material. Extract intelligence across these 9 pillars: 1. STRATEGIC PRIORITIES: Board-level goals and CEO stated direction. Quote their exact language. 2. FINANCIAL SIGNALS: Revenue trends, margin pressure, investment areas, budget reallocations. Flag specific numbers. 3. PAIN POINTS: Stated challenges, risk disclosures, failed initiatives. Note what's buried vs. what's highlighted. 4. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING: How they describe their market position. Who do they benchmark against? 5. TECHNOLOGY DIRECTION: Digital transformation priorities, automation targets, platform investments. 6. REGULATORY & COMPLIANCE: Upcoming deadlines, certification targets, audit findings. 7. PEOPLE & CULTURE: Hiring priorities, skills gaps, leadership changes, organisational restructuring. 8. STAKEHOLDER LANGUAGE: The specific phrases executives use repeatedly. These reveal what their KPIs are tied to. 9. TIMELINE & DEADLINES: Fiscal year, budget cycles, certification deadlines, programme milestones. FORMAT: Structure each pillar with 3-5 key findings. For every claim, cite the source document and location (e.g., "Shareholder Letter, para 3" or "Risk Disclosure, p.47"). Flag anything you cannot verify as "UNVERIFIED — REQUIRES CONFIRMATION."
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Priorities: CEO states "Availability, not just assets." Strategic shift to "Continuous Business Availability."
Pain Points: Micro-outage costs up 12% YoY. Manual incident response creating 47-minute gaps.
Deadlines: ISO 22301 certification targeted for Q3 2026. This creates a deadline-driven procurement event.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

"Availability, not assets" is the CEO's personal framing. Use those exact words and you're an insider. Rephrase it and you sound like a vendor. The ISO 22301 deadline with a skills gap means they need external capability now. Timing matters more than pitch.

02
STEP 2 OF 6

Find the Money

~10 MIN
Most sellers pitch "invest more." Experienced sellers find money the prospect is already wasting. This prompt finds the reallocation budget.
W1-03 // BORING BUT EXPENSIVE — BUDGET DETECTION
Using the company research I've provided, identify "Boring But Expensive" problems — operational costs that are high, visible to the CFO, and solvable but unglamorous. Look for these categories: 1. MANUAL PROCESSES: Workflows requiring human intervention that could be automated. What's the FTE cost? 2. INTEGRATION FRICTION: Data reconciliation between systems, manual handoffs, duplicate entry. Quantify where possible. 3. COMPLIANCE BURDEN: Audit preparation labour, certification maintenance, regulatory reporting overhead. 4. VENDOR DEPENDENCY: Over-reliance on single vendors creating premium pricing or switching costs. 5. TECHNICAL DEBT: End-of-life systems, extended support contracts, legacy maintenance absorbing budget. 6. SKILLS GAPS: Roles that are hard to fill or retain, creating contractor dependency or capability holes. For each finding: — Estimate the annual cost where data supports it — Identify who owns this budget (which department/function) — Frame the reallocation argument: "You're currently spending £X on [boring problem]. Redirecting even a portion toward [solution category] eliminates the root cause." CRITICAL: Do not invent numbers. If you cannot estimate cost from the source material, note "COST UNKNOWN — DISCOVERY QUESTION NEEDED" and suggest the specific question to ask.
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Vendor Dependency: They moved from self-assessment to structured interviews for 230+ vendors quarterly. This is effective but massive labour cost.
Reallocation Arg: "You're spending growing FTEs on manual vendor interviews. A managed resilience framework maintains that depth but frees your team for analysis."

03
STEP 3 OF 6

Profile the People

~15 MIN
Builds a profile beyond job titles. Surfaces decision-making style and the "One Question" that proves you understand their personal agenda.
W1-05 // EXECUTIVE STRATEGIC INTELLIGENCE
Analyse the following LinkedIn profile, recent posts, and any available public commentary for [NAME], [TITLE] at [COMPANY]. Cross-reference against the company research already provided. Build a profile covering: 1. ROLE & RESPONSIBILITIES: What do they actually own? Budget size? Team size? Who do they report to? 2. STATED PRIORITIES: What themes dominate their public commentary? What do they post about, share, and comment on? 3. DECISION-MAKING STYLE: Evidence-based or intuitive? Collaborative or directive? Risk-averse or progressive? Cite specific examples from their content. 4. PROFESSIONAL REPUTATION: How do colleagues describe them? What do endorsements and recommendations reveal about how they operate? 5. PERSONAL DIMENSIONS: Interests outside work mentioned in profile or posts. Community involvement. Communication style quirks. 6. THE ONE QUESTION: Based on everything above, give me ONE discovery question that demonstrates I understand their personal agenda — not just the company's priorities. This question should make them think: "This person has done their homework." FORMAT: Structured profile. For every claim, cite the source (LinkedIn post date, recommendation author, etc.). Flag assumptions clearly.
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE (JENNIFER MORRIS)

Priorities: "Resilience is revenue protection." Shares her failures publicly (e.g., the 47-min gap). Values honesty over polish.
The One Question: "You've publicly shared the stress-test findings including the 47-minute gap. That level of transparency is unusual. How has that openness shaped the way your Risk Committee engages with resilience reporting?"

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Jennifer shares failure publicly. That tells you she values honesty. Lead with what your solution doesn't do well. A "Voss Accusation Audit" opener acknowledges the hard truth before she raises it.

04
STEP 4 OF 6

Synthesise Battle Card

~10 MIN
Turns intelligence into ammunition. A single-page reference for the meeting: Hooks, Landmines, and the "North Star".
W1-08 // BATTLE CARD SYNTHESIS
Using all the intelligence gathered so far — company research, budget signals, and stakeholder profile — create a 1-page Executive Battle Card. Structure: 1. CFO NORTH STAR: The single strategic priority that defines this company's direction right now. Use their exact language. 2. THE THREE HOOKS: Three conversation-opening insights derived from the research. Each hook must: — Reference a specific finding from the research (with source) — Connect to a capability you could provide — Be framed as an observation, not a pitch ("I noticed..." not "We offer...") 3. ACCUSATION AUDIT (Voss framework): Write 3 statements that acknowledge the prospect's likely concerns before they raise them. Format: "You're probably thinking [concern]. Here's what I'd want to know if I were in your position..." 4. BOARDROOM QUESTION: One question designed for a board-level audience that demonstrates strategic understanding of their 12-24 month roadmap. This question should be impossible to ask without having done this research. 5. PILLAR 9 INTEGRATION: How does your potential solution connect to their stated strategic direction? Map capability to their language, not yours. 6. LANDMINES: What topics should you AVOID? What past failures or sensitivities should you navigate carefully?
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

North Star: "Continuous Business Availability."
Hook 1: "Your stress-test showed a 47-minute gap in recovery transition. That pattern is common in manual-to-automated shifts."
Landmine: Do NOT reference the failed automation project from the risk disclosure directly. Use it to shape your "managed risk" approach, but don't quote it.

05
STEP 5 OF 6

Research YOUR Company

~10 MIN
Don't pitch generic capabilities. Match your proof points to their specific pain points and language.
W1-09 // CAPABILITY MATCHING
Based on the company research, stakeholder profile, and battle card I've provided, search the following materials from MY company for the best matches: [PASTE: Your company's case studies, solution descriptions, capability statements, or marketing materials] Find: 1. CASE STUDIES: Which client stories are most relevant to this prospect's specific situation? Match on industry, challenge type, scale, and outcomes. 2. PROOF POINTS: Specific metrics or outcomes from our work that directly address their stated pain points. 3. LANGUAGE ALIGNMENT: Where do our materials already use their language? Where do we need to translate? (e.g., if they say "availability" and we say "uptime" — flag the disconnect.) 4. GAPS: Where does this prospect need something we don't have a clear proof point for? These become discovery questions, not weaknesses to hide. 5. SLIDE RELEVANCY: If I have a standard presentation deck, which slides should I INCLUDE, CUT, or MODIFY for this specific meeting? FORMAT: Prioritised list. Most relevant match first. For each, explain WHY it's relevant to THIS prospect — not why it's good in general.

SECURITY WARNING

This prompt requires inputting internal company data. Do NOT paste proprietary material into public models. Use your firm's secure internal AI (e.g., Copilot Enterprise) or modify the prompt to search only your public-facing case studies.

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Language Flag: Our materials say "disaster recovery." Meridian says "Continuous Business Availability." Philosophically different. Reframe everything.
Gap: No direct proof point for "resilience capability training." Turn this into a discovery question: "Is capability building a long-term internal goal?"

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

If you say "disaster recovery" when she says "availability," you've revealed you don't understand her strategy. The AI catches this disconnect. Junior sellers don't.

06
STEP 6 OF 6

The Deliverable

~15 MIN
The output isn't a research doc. It's an executive briefing you review on your phone 10 minutes before the meeting.
W1-14 // EXECUTIVE BRIEFING GENERATOR
Using everything from Steps 1-5, compile a complete Executive Briefing for my meeting with [NAME] at [COMPANY]. Structure the briefing as follows: SECTION 1 — STAKEHOLDER PROFILE Name, title, reporting line, budget authority, team size. Decision-making style summary. Personal dimensions relevant to rapport-building. SECTION 2 — COMPANY CONTEXT Strategic direction in 3 sentences using their language. Key pain points with severity ranking. Hard deadlines driving urgency. SECTION 3 — HOW WE GOT HERE The pathway to this meeting. Who introduced us? What's the relationship chain? What do they already know about us? SECTION 4 — MEETING OBJECTIVES Primary objective: The one outcome that makes this meeting a success. Secondary objective: The fallback — what's the minimum acceptable outcome? Discovery questions: The 3 questions I must ask, ranked by priority. SECTION 5 — PERSONAL DIMENSIONS Interests, communication style, rapport opportunities. Things to reference naturally, not as a scripted icebreaker. SECTION 6 — KEY MESSAGING The 3 hooks from the battle card, refined into conversational language. The accusation audit statements, ready to deploy. The boardroom question. What to AVOID. SECTION 7 — PROOF POINTS Relevant case studies and metrics mapped to their priorities. Language alignment notes. This briefing should be ready to review on a phone screen 10 minutes before walking into the room. Concise. Actionable. No filler.
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Objective: Secure follow-up with CRO regarding ISO 22301 deadline.
Messaging: Hook 1 (47-min gap), Hook 2 (12% cost increase), Hook 3 (Vendor Assessment scale).
Boardroom Question: "How is the Steering Committee balancing internal capability vs. external managed services for the ISO 22301 deadline?"

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The briefing isn't a script; it's a confidence engine. When you know the 'How We Got Here' and 'Personal Dimensions' cold, you stop thinking about your notes and start listening to the room. That presence is what wins the deal.

C1
COMPACT VARIANT

9-Pillar Compact Research

~10 MIN
W1-02 // 9-PILLAR COMPACT
Act as a Market Intelligence Analyst preparing a rapid pre-meeting brief. I will provide: an annual report or company website content plus any available press releases. Extract intelligence across the same 9 pillars as the deep research prompt, but in COMPACT format: 1. STRATEGIC PRIORITIES: Top 3 board-level goals in their language. One sentence each. 2. FINANCIAL SIGNALS: Key numbers — revenue trend, margin direction, investment areas. 3-5 data points. 3. PAIN POINTS: Top 3 stated challenges. What keeps the CEO up at night? 4. COMPETITIVE POSITIONING: Who do they benchmark against? One sentence. 5. TECHNOLOGY DIRECTION: Top 2 technology bets. What are they building toward? 6. REGULATORY: Any upcoming deadlines or compliance pressures? 7. PEOPLE: Key hiring or restructuring signals? 8. STAKEHOLDER LANGUAGE: The 3-5 phrases executives repeat most. These are KPI-linked. 9. TIMELINE: Next 12 months — what deadlines or milestones are approaching? FORMAT: Bullet points. Maximum 3 per pillar. No pillar should exceed 4 lines. Flag anything unverified. This briefing should be readable in 3 minutes.
WHERE THIS BREAKS

FAILURE 1: Hallucination

AI fabricated a partnership between two companies. The battle card referenced it. If it had reached the client, credibility would be zero.

RULE: Use the Verification Prompt (W1-13). If you can't see the source, it doesn't exist.

FAILURE 2: Over-reliance

Copied the briefing output directly into an email. The language was correct but generic. The recipient ignored it.

RULE: AI generates 80%. The last 20%—the humanity—is yours.

FAILURE 3: False Confidence

Battle card was perfect, but the CFO had left the company 3 months prior. The AI didn't know because the annual report was 9 months old.

RULE: Verify leadership changes within 48 hours of the meeting.
