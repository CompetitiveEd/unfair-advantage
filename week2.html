WEEK 2 — [A] ASSESS / MAP THEM — OMNISCIENCE ENGINE

You mapped one person. Now map the deal.

From first stakeholder to full authority map. The political intelligence engine that wins complex deals.

The Context

Sarah won the first meeting. The Director loved the approach. Three months later, she lost the deal. Not because the solution was wrong, but because she built her strategy around one person. The CIO had different priorities. The CFO wanted a different model.

Week 2 exists because complex deals are won by coalitions, not champions.

Completed Week 1? This week builds directly on Week 1 intelligence. If you haven't run the Intelligence Layer prompts, start there.

The Omniscience Flow

Steps 1-3 extract intelligence. Steps 4-5 build the map. Steps 6-7 turn it into strategy.

01
STEP 1 OF 10

Decode the Opportunity

~20 MIN
Every RFP tells two stories: the official requirements and the hidden pain points. This prompt reads both.
W2-01 // RFP STRATEGIC DECODER
Act as an expert Proposal Director and Strategic Deal Analyst. I am providing you with the text from an RFP / Opportunity Description for [INSERT CLIENT NAME]. Your task is to decode the "story behind the document." Analyze the text and produce a 'Strategic Decoding Table' with the following columns: 1. EXPLICIT REQUIREMENT: What they strictly asked for. 2. IMPLICIT SIGNAL: What the language intensity, specificity, or repetition suggests is the real driver. 3. LIKELY PAIN POINT: What specifically went wrong with the incumbent to cause this requirement to be written this way. 4. RISK LEVEL: (Low/Med/High) How dangerous this is if we get it wrong. Then, determine the 'Trigger Event': Based on the text, was this initiated by: A) Contract Expiry (Commodity mindset, looking for price/efficiency) B) Regulatory/Compliance Failure (Risk-averse, looking for safety) C) New Strategic Mandate (Transformation mindset, looking for innovation) Explain your reasoning. [PASTE RFP / OPPORTUNITY TEXT HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Trigger Event: Not simple contract expiry. It's a triple trigger: Board Mandate (Meridian 2030) + Regulatory Pressure (PRA Resilience) + Operational Pain (Legacy infra).

Decoding: The narrative emphasis on "outcome-linked pricing" contradicts the 30% commercial weighting. The CIO's letter signals that commercial innovation matters more than the scoring table suggests.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The trigger question is the most important. Expiry = commodity mindset. Regulatory = risk-averse. Board mandate = strategic. The same RFP reads completely differently once you know which triggered it.

02
STEP 2 OF 10

Find the People

~15 MIN
Builds the complete stakeholder register and—critically—identifies who is MISSING.
W2-02 // STAKEHOLDER DISCOVERY
Act as a Strategic Account Manager. I am providing you with [INSERT SOURCE MATERIAL: RFP text, meeting notes, LinkedIn exports]. 1. Extract every individual person named or referenced. 2. Build a 'Stakeholder Register' table with columns: Name, Job Title, Role in Deal (Decision Maker/Influencer/Evaluator), Source of Info. 3. Crucially, create a 'Missing Stakeholders' list. Based on a typical deal of this size and complexity ([INSERT DEAL SIZE/TYPE]), who is missing? (e.g., Is there a Procurement lead? A Privacy Officer? A CFO sponsor?). 4. For the missing roles, suggest the likely Job Titles we should search for on LinkedIn. [PASTE SOURCE MATERIAL HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Register should find 8+ names. The RFP only names Sarah Blackwell. LinkedIn/Website reveals Hale (CIO), Harrison (COO), Morris (Resilience).

Missing: Who sits on the Risk Committee? Who approves the contract? Who is the TUPE lead?

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The research priority ranking is the real output. You can't profile everyone equally — time is finite. Influence × intelligence gap tells you where to invest. High influence, low knowledge = urgent.

03
STEP 3 OF 10

Decode Requirements

~15 MIN
Every unusually specific requirement was born in a bad experience. This prompt does the archaeology.
W2-03 // REQUIREMENTS DECODER
Analyze the requirements section of this RFP. Group the requirements into 'Clusters of Density' — areas where there are an unusually high number of specific, detailed, or punitive requirements. For each Cluster: 1. Name the 'Trauma': What specific operational failure or vendor issue likely happened in the past to make them write this section so aggressively? 2. Define our 'Response Strategy': Should we: - COMPLY (Standard response is fine) - DIFFERENTIATE (This is a major win theme opportunity) - MITIGATE (This is a trap or high-risk area) Output as a table. [PASTE REQUIREMENTS SECTION HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Resilience Cluster: "Regulatory notification workflows" implies they've been burned by a regulatory audit.

Automation Cluster: "Reduce alert noise by >70%" is specific. They know their current 14,000 alerts are unmanageable.

Trauma: A vendor who delivered bodies, not outcomes.

04
STEP 4 OF 10

Map Motivations

~20 MIN
What failure looks like for them specifically. The difference between knowing the org chart and knowing the politics.
W2-04 // STAKEHOLDER MOTIVATION MAP
Act as an expert in Chris Voss's negotiation tactics and Cialdini's influence principles. I am providing the profile for [STAKEHOLDER NAME], [JOB TITLE]. Generate a 'Deep Motivation Profile': 1. The 'Black Swan': What is the one piece of information that, if we knew it, would change how we approach them? (Hypothesize based on role/industry). 2. Stated vs. Hidden Drivers: What do they say they want (e.g., Innovation) vs. what their role/bonus actually rewards (e.g., Risk Reduction). 3. The 'No-Oriented' Question: Draft a question designed to get them to say "No" to reveal their constraints (e.g., "Is it ridiculous to suggest that cost certainty is more important than feature breadth right now?"). 4. Success Definition: Complete this sentence for them: "I will be promoted/bonused next year if this project delivers..." [PASTE STAKEHOLDER PROFILE/LINKEDIN DATA HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Stated vs Hidden: Harrison posts about operational excellence. But his hidden driver is the Meridian 2030 delivery milestones — his performance is tied to transforming operations without breaking production. He needs discipline, not experiments.

The Black Swan: His Post #4 says explicitly: "We're not outsourcing this to a consulting firm." This is a BLOCKER signal. If your proposal reads like a consulting pitch, he will kill it.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

A CIO who posts about 'innovation' but whose bonus is tied to 'cost reduction' will evaluate your proposal through the cost lens. Follow the incentives, not the LinkedIn posts.

05
STEP 5 OF 10

Map Authority

~20 MIN
The single most important output. Formal decision structure AND informal influence lines.
W2-05 // MULTI-STAKEHOLDER AUTHORITY MAP
Synthesize all the stakeholder intelligence we have gathered so far. Create a 'Political Authority Map' for this deal: 1. Classify each stakeholder into Miller Heiman roles: - Economic Buyer (Releases budget) - User Buyer (Uses the solution) - Technical Buyer (Judges the spec/compliance) - Coach (Guides us) 2. Map the 'Influence Network': Who influences whom? (e.g., "CFO likely defers to CTO on technical risk, but holds veto on commercial terms"). 3. Identify the 'Winning Coalition': Which minimum set of 3-4 people, if they all vote 'Yes', makes the deal inevitable? 4. Identify the 'Blockers': Who has the power to kill this deal, and what is their likely objection? [PASTE ALL STAKEHOLDER SUMMARIES HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Winning Coalition: Hale + Morris + Thompson. If those three support you, Osei follows and Harrison's objections become manageable.

Blocker: David Harrison (COO) — outsourcing scepticism, zero-disruption requirement.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The winning coalition is the strategic core. You don't need everyone. You need the MINIMUM set of stakeholders who, if they all support you, make the decision inevitable.

06
STEP 6 OF 10

Plan Engagement

~15 MIN
Specific value propositions, specific discovery questions, specific messaging guidance for each person.
W2-06 // PERSONALISED ENGAGEMENT PLANS
Create a 'Strategic Engagement Card' for [STAKEHOLDER NAME]. Structure it as a meeting preparation guide: 1. The Hook (First 2 mins): A specific insight or 'Accusation Audit' opener that proves we understand their world immediately. 2. The Discovery Goal: What is the ONE piece of information we must learn from them in the next interaction? 3. The Value Drop: What specific insight, data point, or case study should we share that helps them achieve THEIR personal goal (from the Motivation Map)? 4. The Ask: What is the calibrated next step we should ask for? [PASTE STAKEHOLDER MOTIVATION PROFILE HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Harrison: Lead with operational data, transition risk mitigation, zero-disruption guarantees. Don't say "innovation."

Morris: Lead with resilience peer credibility and transparency.

Hale: Lead with technical architecture and cloud migration proof points.

07
STEP 7 OF 10

Deal Strategy

~15 MIN
Everything synthesised into one page. If you can't fit your deal strategy on one page, you don't understand the deal well enough.
W2-07 // DEAL STRATEGY SYNTHESIS
Synthesize everything into a 'One-Page Deal Strategy' (Plan on a Page). Sections: 1. Executive Summary: The deal in 3 sentences (Context, Opportunity, Why Us). 2. The Winning Coalition: The 3-4 people we are targeting. 3. Three Key Win Themes: The strategic pillars of our bid. 4. Top 3 Risks & Mitigations. 5. Pricing Strategy Headline. 6. The 'No-Bid' Criteria: Under what specific conditions should we walk away from this deal? [PASTE ALL PREVIOUS OUTPUTS HERE]
MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Win Themes: "Right size, right focus" + "Resilience as operational DNA" + "Commercial alignment through shared risk."

No-Bid Criteria: Walk away if Harrison has already selected an incumbent extension or if evaluation is weighted >50% on price.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The no-bid criteria at the bottom is the section most people skip and most deals need. Under what circumstances do you walk away? If you can't answer that question, you haven't qualified the deal.

08
STEP 8 OF 10

Cross-Stakeholder Dynamics

ADVANCED
W2-08 // CROSS-STAKEHOLDER DYNAMICS MAP
Synthesise all individual stakeholder profiles (minimum 3). Map how stakeholders INTERACT — not just what each wants individually. 1. ALIGNMENT MAP: Where do priorities agree across stakeholders? What shared language appears? 2. TENSION MAP: Where do priorities conflict? (e.g., CFO wants cost certainty, CIO wants transformation investment, COO wants zero disruption). 3. INFLUENCE FLOW: Who defers to whom on what topics? Who has informal authority? 4. COALITION SCENARIOS: If stakeholder X champions you, who follows? If stakeholder Y blocks, what happens? 5. THE QUIET STAKEHOLDER: Who has been silent? Absence of signal IS a signal. [PASTE ALL STAKEHOLDER PROFILES HERE]
09
STEP 9 OF 10

Culture & Values Alignment

ADVANCED
W2-09 // CULTURE & VALUES ALIGNMENT
Assess cultural compatibility between your organisation and the prospect for a long-term partnership. Using: prospect website culture/values, careers page, leadership LinkedIn posts, employee reviews. 1. VALUES ALIGNMENT: Map their stated values against yours. Where is genuine overlap? 2. WORKING STYLE COMPATIBILITY: Formal or informal? Data-driven or narrative? Hierarchical or flat? 3. CULTURAL SIGNALS FROM THE OPPORTUNITY: What does the RFP reveal about expectations? ("Partnership" = collaboration. "Governance" = accountability.) 4. EVIDENCE FOR THE PROPOSAL: Specific cultural alignment proof points to embed. 5. CULTURAL RISKS: Where might friction emerge? [PASTE CULTURAL MATERIALS FROM BOTH ORGANISATIONS]

STEP 10 OF 10

Human Risk & TUPE

ADVANCED
W2-10 // THE HUMAN RISK & TUPE MAPPER
ROLE: You are a Human Capital Strategist and TUPE Expert. You are mapping the emotional and operational risks of a people transfer. 1. THE FEAR FACTORS: Identify the top 3 anxieties for the transferred staff (e.g., "Will I have a job in 12 months?"). 2. THE KNOWLEDGE GAP: Identify which specific roles hold the "institutional DNA." If these people leave, what breaks? 3. THE "VALUE MIRROR": Map the client's stated people values directly to your own. 4. THE DAY 1 PLAN: Draft the "First 24 Hours" engagement plan for the transferred staff. [PASTE EMPLOYEE DATA AND CULTURAL RESEARCH]
E
INTERLUDE

The Engineering Layer

OPTIONAL
Generic pricing will kill this bid. These prompts translate technical complexity into commercial safety.
E-01 // THE 'PLACEMAT' VISUALIZER
ROLE: You are a Strategic Visualiser and Enterprise Architect. Goal: Synthesise complex transformation into a SINGLE visual "placemat". 1. THE "FROM" STATE: Visual metaphor for current pain (e.g., "Tangled wires"). 2. THE "TO" STATE: Visual metaphor for solution (e.g., "Streamlined highway"). 3. THE BRIDGE: How do we visually represent the journey? 4. THE "YES" LABELS: Specific client terms that must appear. 5. CAPTIONING: Single sentence caption summarizing ROI. [PASTE CLIENT STATE AND GOALS]
E-02 // THE LOGIC ENGINE
ROLE: Technical Solution Architect. Need a "Decision Logic Matrix" to categorize complexity. 1. COMPLEXITY DRIVERS: Identify 5-7 factors (e.g., Legacy OS, Dongles). 2. THE DECISION TREE: IF [Condition A] THEN [Action B] (Cost = X). 3. CLASSIFICATION MATRIX: Define Simple / Medium / Complex / Toxic. 4. SCORING MECHANISM: Assign risk scores to unknowns. [PASTE TECHNICAL SCOPE]
E-03 // THE COMMERCIAL SHIELD
ROLE: Commercial Risk Officer. Protect from "Black Hole" risks. 1. THE "SILENT KILLERS": Requirements that look simple but have infinite cost depth. 2. DEFENSIVE DEFINITIONS: In Scope vs Out of Scope vs Dependencies. 3. DISCLAIMER: Define pricing as "Indicative based on assumptions". 4. ASSUMPTION LOG: 5 critical assumptions that trigger Change Control. [PASTE RFP SCOPE]
E-04 // THE COMMERCIAL LEVER ENGINE
ROLE: Commercial Architect. Move from fixed price to "price sliders". 1. THE LEVERS: Identify 3-5 variables (e.g., Service Window, Location, Speed). 2. THE TRADE-OFFS: Cost Impact vs Risk Impact for each. 3. RECOMMENDATION: The "Balanced" starting point. 4. THE DIALOGUE: Script for the "Levers Conversation". [PASTE SOLUTION AND BUDGET]
WHERE THIS BREAKS

FAILURE 1: The Invisible Stakeholder

Won the technical evaluation. Lost at final board. A non-executive director — never mentioned in any document — had a prior relationship with the competitor's CEO.

RULE: Your stakeholder map is never complete. Always ask: "Who ISN'T on the list that should be?"

FAILURE 2: The Coalition That Wasn't

Mapped 8 stakeholders. Discovered at orals that the CIO and CFO had fundamentally different visions for the programme. Our proposal forced the confrontation. They chose to delay rather than decide.

RULE: If two stakeholders both say "yes" but mean different things, you have a time bomb.

FAILURE 3: The Map That Decayed

Built a map in January. Submitted in April. The procurement director had moved teams in February. Three months of strategy built on an org chart that no longer existed.

RULE: Refresh within 2 weeks of any deadline.
