01
Decode the Opportunity
~20 MIN STRATEGIC BID ANALYSIS

Every procurement document tells two stories. The official story is in the requirements and evaluation criteria. The real story is in the language intensity, the specificity of certain requirements, the gaps, and the timeline compression. This prompt reads both.

W2-01 // RFP STRATEGIC DECODER
Act as an expert Proposal Director and Strategic Deal Analyst.

I am providing you with the text from an RFP / Opportunity Description for [INSERT CLIENT NAME].

Your task is to decode the "story behind the document." 

Analyze the text and produce a 'Strategic Decoding Table' with the following columns:
1. EXPLICIT REQUIREMENT: What they strictly asked for.
2. IMPLICIT SIGNAL: What the language intensity, specificity, or repetition suggests is the real driver.
3. LIKELY PAIN POINT: What specifically went wrong with the incumbent to cause this requirement to be written this way.
4. RISK LEVEL: (Low/Med/High) How dangerous this is if we get it wrong.

Then, determine the 'Trigger Event': Based on the text, was this initiated by:
A) Contract Expiry (Commodity mindset, looking for price/efficiency)
B) Regulatory/Compliance Failure (Risk-averse, looking for safety)
C) New Strategic Mandate (Transformation mindset, looking for innovation)
Explain your reasoning.

WHERE THIS BREAKS

Honest Failures

Three ways I have messed this up, so you don't have to.

FAILURE 1: The Invisible Stakeholder

Built a comprehensive authority map for a 6-person evaluation panel. Won the written submission. Lost at orals. The CEO's EA â€” never mentioned in any document â€” had been asked for her opinion on "which team she'd want to work with." She preferred the competitor because they'd been more personable with the support staff during the site visit.

RULE: Your stakeholder map is never complete. Always ask: "Who else will have an opinion on this?"

FAILURE 2: The Stale Map

Mapped stakeholders in January. Submitted the bid in March. The CIO had been replaced in February. The new CIO had different priorities. The entire deal strategy was built on a person who no longer existed in the role.

RULE: Stakeholder maps decay. Refresh within 2 weeks of any submission deadline.

FAILURE 3: Over-Engineering the Politics

Spent 6 hours building a detailed authority map for a deal that turned out to be a single-decision-maker procurement. The CEO was making the decision himself based on a 30-minute meeting. All the political intelligence was irrelevant.

RULE: Match preparation to decision complexity. Not every deal needs Week 2 depth.
