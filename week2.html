THE FRAMEWORK

DECODE OPPORTUNITY → FIND PEOPLE → MAP MOTIVATIONS → MAP AUTHORITY → DEAL STRATEGY

01
STEP 1 OF 10

Decode the Opportunity

~20 MIN

Every RFP tells two stories: the official requirements and the hidden pain points. This prompt reads both.

W2-01 // RFP STRATEGIC DECODER
Act as an expert Proposal Director and Strategic Deal Analyst.

I am providing you with the text from an RFP / Opportunity Description for [INSERT CLIENT NAME].

Your task is to decode the "story behind the document." 

Analyze the text and produce a 'Strategic Decoding Table' with the following columns:
1. EXPLICIT REQUIREMENT: What they strictly asked for.
2. IMPLICIT SIGNAL: What the language intensity, specificity, or repetition suggests is the real driver.
3. LIKELY PAIN POINT: What specifically went wrong with the incumbent to cause this requirement to be written this way.
4. RISK LEVEL: (Low/Med/High) How dangerous this is if we get it wrong.

Then, determine the 'Trigger Event': Based on the text, was this initiated by:
A) Contract Expiry (Commodity mindset, looking for price/efficiency)
B) Regulatory/Compliance Failure (Risk-averse, looking for safety)
C) New Strategic Mandate (Transformation mindset, looking for innovation)
Explain your reasoning.

[PASTE RFP / OPPORTUNITY TEXT HERE]
+

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE
Trigger Event: Not simple contract expiry. It's a triple trigger: Board Mandate (Meridian 2030) + Regulatory Pressure (PRA Resilience) + Operational Pain (Legacy infra).
Decoding: The narrative emphasis on "outcome-linked pricing" contradicts the 30% commercial weighting. The CIO's letter signals that commercial innovation matters more than the scoring table suggests.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES — The trigger question is the most important. Expiry = commodity mindset. Regulatory = risk-averse. Board mandate = strategic. The same RFP reads completely differently once you know which triggered it.

02
STEP 2 OF 10

Find the People

~15 MIN

Builds the complete stakeholder register and—critically—identifies who is MISSING.

W2-02 // STAKEHOLDER DISCOVERY
Act as a Strategic Account Manager.

I am providing you with [INSERT SOURCE MATERIAL: RFP text, meeting notes, LinkedIn exports].

1. Extract every individual person named or referenced.
2. Build a 'Stakeholder Register' table with columns: Name, Job Title, Role in Deal (Decision Maker/Influencer/Evaluator), Source of Info.
3. Crucially, create a 'Missing Stakeholders' list. Based on a typical deal of this size and complexity ([INSERT DEAL SIZE/TYPE]), who is missing? (e.g., Is there a Procurement lead? A Privacy Officer? A CFO sponsor?). 
4. For the missing roles, suggest the likely Job Titles we should search for on LinkedIn.

[PASTE SOURCE MATERIAL HERE]
+

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE
Register should find 8+ names. The RFP only names Sarah Blackwell. LinkedIn/Website reveals Hale (CIO), Harrison (COO), Morris (Resilience).
Missing: Who sits on the Risk Committee? Who approves the contract? Who is the TUPE lead?

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES — The research priority ranking is the real output. You can't profile everyone equally — time is finite. Influence × intelligence gap tells you where to invest. High influence, low knowledge = urgent. Low influence, high knowledge = parked.

03
STEP 3 OF 10

Decode Requirements

~15 MIN

Every unusually specific requirement was born in a bad experience. This prompt does the archaeology.

W2-03 // REQUIREMENTS DECODER
Analyze the requirements section of this RFP.

Group the requirements into 'Clusters of Density' — areas where there are an unusually high number of specific, detailed, or punitive requirements.

For each Cluster:
1. Name the 'Trauma': What specific operational failure or vendor issue likely happened in the past to make them write this section so aggressively?
2. Define our 'Response Strategy': Should we:
   - COMPLY (Standard response is fine)
   - DIFFERENTIATE (This is a major win theme opportunity)
   - MITIGATE (This is a trap or high-risk area)

Output as a table.

[PASTE REQUIREMENTS SECTION HERE]
+

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE
Resilience Cluster: "Regulatory notification workflows" implies they've been burned by a regulatory audit.
Automation Cluster: "Reduce alert noise by >70%" is specific. They know their current 14,000 alerts are unmanageable.
Trauma: A vendor who delivered bodies, not outcomes.

04
STEP 4 OF 10

Map Motivations

~20 MIN

What failure looks like for them specifically. The difference between knowing the org chart and knowing the politics.

W2-04 // STAKEHOLDER MOTIVATION MAP
Act as an expert in Chris Voss's negotiation tactics and Cialdini's influence principles.

I am providing the profile for [STAKEHOLDER NAME], [JOB TITLE].

Generate a 'Deep Motivation Profile':
1. The 'Black Swan': What is the one piece of information that, if we knew it, would change how we approach them? (Hypothesize based on role/industry).
2. Stated vs. Hidden Drivers: What do they say they want (e.g., Innovation) vs. what their role/bonus actually rewards (e.g., Risk Reduction).
3. The 'No-Oriented' Question: Draft a question designed to get them to say "No" to reveal their constraints (e.g., "Is it ridiculous to suggest that cost certainty is more important than feature breadth right now?").
4. Success Definition: Complete this sentence for them: "I will be promoted/bonused next year if this project delivers..."

[PASTE STAKEHOLDER PROFILE/LINKEDIN DATA HERE]
+

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE
Feed David Harrison's LinkedIn profile into this prompt alongside the RFP and company research. The motivation map should surface:

Stated vs Hidden: Harrison posts about operational excellence. But his hidden driver is the Meridian 2030 delivery milestones — his performance is tied to transforming operations without breaking production. He needs discipline, not experiments.
The Black Swan: His Post #4 says explicitly: "We're not outsourcing this to a consulting firm." This is a BLOCKER signal. If your proposal reads like a consulting pitch, he will kill it.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES — A CIO who posts about 'innovation' but whose bonus is tied to 'cost reduction' will evaluate your proposal through the cost lens. Follow the incentives, not the LinkedIn posts.

STEP 5 OF 10

Map Authority

~20 MIN

The single most important output. Formal decision structure AND informal influence lines.

W2-05 // MULTI-STAKEHOLDER AUTHORITY MAP
Synthesize all the stakeholder intelligence we have gathered so far.

Create a 'Political Authority Map' for this deal:

1. Classify each stakeholder into Miller Heiman roles:
   - Economic Buyer (Releases budget)
   - User Buyer (Uses the solution)
   - Technical Buyer (Judges the spec/compliance)
   - Coach (Guides us)

2. Map the 'Influence Network': Who influences whom? (e.g., "CFO likely defers to CTO on technical risk, but holds veto on commercial terms").

3. Identify the 'Winning Coalition': Which minimum set of 3-4 people, if they all vote 'Yes', makes the deal inevitable?

4. Identify the 'Blockers': Who has the power to kill this deal, and what is their likely objection?

[PASTE ALL STAKEHOLDER SUMMARIES HERE]
+

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE
Winning Coalition: Hale + Morris + Thompson. If those three support you, Osei follows and Harrison's objections become manageable.
Blocker: David Harrison (COO) — outsourcing scepticism, zero-disruption requirement.
If your map doesn't identify Harrison as a blocker risk, you haven't read his posts carefully enough.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES — The winning coalition is the strategic core. You don't need everyone. You need the MINIMUM set of stakeholders who, if they all support you, make the decision inevitable. Identify the linchpin — the person who unlocks the others.

Plan Engagement

~15 MIN

Specific value propositions, specific discovery questions, specific messaging guidance for each person.

W2-06 // PERSONALISED ENGAGEMENT PLANS
Create a 'Strategic Engagement Card' for [STAKEHOLDER NAME].

Structure it as a meeting preparation guide:

1. The Hook (First 2 mins): A specific insight or 'Accusation Audit' opener that proves we understand their world immediately.
2. The Discovery Goal: What is the ONE piece of information we must learn from them in the next interaction?
3. The Value Drop: What specific insight, data point, or case study should we share that helps them achieve THEIR personal goal (from the Motivation Map)?
4. The Ask: What is the calibrated next step we should ask for?

[PASTE STAKEHOLDER MOTIVATION PROFILE HERE]
+

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE
Harrison: Lead with operational data, transition risk mitigation, zero-disruption guarantees. Don't say "innovation."
Morris: Lead with resilience peer credibility and transparency.
Hale: Lead with technical architecture and cloud migration proof points.
If all four cards use the same messaging, you haven't understood the stakeholders.

STEP 7 OF 10

Deal Strategy

~15 MIN

Everything synthesised into one page. If you can't fit your deal strategy on one page, you don't understand the deal well enough.

W2-07 // DEAL STRATEGY SYNTHESIS
Synthesize everything into a 'One-Page Deal Strategy' (Plan on a Page).

Sections:
1. Executive Summary: The deal in 3 sentences (Context, Opportunity, Why Us).
2. The Winning Coalition: The 3-4 people we are targeting.
3. Three Key Win Themes: The strategic pillars of our bid.
4. Top 3 Risks & Mitigations.
5. Pricing Strategy Headline.
6. The 'No-Bid' Criteria: Under what specific conditions should we walk away from this deal?

[PASTE ALL PREVIOUS OUTPUTS HERE]
+

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE
Win Themes: "Right size, right focus" + "Resilience as operational DNA" + "Commercial alignment through shared risk."
No-Bid Criteria: Walk away if Harrison has already selected an incumbent extension or if evaluation is weighted >50% on price.
If your deal strategy doesn't have no-bid criteria, you haven't qualified the deal.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES — The no-bid criteria at the bottom is the section most people skip and most deals need. Under what circumstances do you walk away? If you can't answer that question, you haven't qualified the deal.

Cross-Stakeholder Dynamics

ADVANCED

Understand how they interact. Where do priorities conflict? Who defers to whom?

W2-08 // CROSS-STAKEHOLDER DYNAMICS MAP
Synthesise all individual stakeholder profiles (minimum 3). Map how stakeholders INTERACT — not just what each wants individually.

1. ALIGNMENT MAP: Where do priorities agree across stakeholders? What shared language appears?
2. TENSION MAP: Where do priorities conflict? (e.g., CFO wants cost certainty, CIO wants transformation investment, COO wants zero disruption).
3. INFLUENCE FLOW: Who defers to whom on what topics? Who has informal authority?
4. COALITION SCENARIOS: If stakeholder X champions you, who follows? If stakeholder Y blocks, what happens?
5. THE QUIET STAKEHOLDER: Who has been silent? Absence of signal IS a signal.

W2-09 // CULTURE & VALUES ALIGNMENT
Assess cultural compatibility between your organisation and the prospect for a long-term partnership.

ROLE: You are a Human Capital Strategist and TUPE Expert. You are mapping the emotional and operational risks of a people transfer.

1. THE FEAR FACTORS: Identify the top 3 anxieties for the transferred staff (e.g., "Will I have a job in 12 months?").

3. THE "VALUE MIRROR": Map the client's stated people values directly to your own.
4. THE DAY 1 PLAN: Draft the "First 24 Hours" engagement plan for the transferred staff.

WHERE THIS BREAKS

FAILURE 1: The Invisible Stakeholder

Won the technical evaluation. Lost at final board. A non-executive director — never mentioned in any document, never in any meeting — had a prior relationship with the competitor's CEO. The deal was decided in a conversation we didn't know existed, between people we didn't know mattered.

RULE: Your stakeholder map is never complete. The question isn't "who's on the list?" — it's "who ISN'T on the list that should be?" Always ask your champion: "Who else has a voice in this decision that I haven't met?"

FAILURE 2: The Coalition That Wasn't

Mapped 8 stakeholders. Identified the winning coalition. Built tailored messaging for each. Discovered at orals that the CIO and CFO had fundamentally different visions for the programme — and had been avoiding the conversation. Our proposal forced the confrontation. They chose to delay rather than decide.

RULE: A coalition requires alignment, not just individual support. If two stakeholders both say "yes" but mean different things by it, you don't have a coalition — you have a time bomb. Map the tensions, not just the support.

FAILURE 3: The Map That Decayed

Built a beautiful authority map in January. Submitted in April. The procurement director had moved teams in February. The new person had no relationship with us, no context, and a different evaluation methodology. Three months of strategy built on an org chart that no longer existed.

RULE: Stakeholder maps are perishable goods. Refresh within 2 weeks of any deadline. If you can't verify a stakeholder is still in role, your map is a guess.
