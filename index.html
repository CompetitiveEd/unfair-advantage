How Research Won a Major Deal

Tom is an experienced Account Executive. Over a decade in role. Recently lost a significant deal to a competitor.

The feedback stung: "They seemed to understand our business better."

Tom was frustrated. He knew this sector. He understood the dynamics, the pressures, the decision-making patterns. But when he looked at the winning pitch, he saw it: the competitor had quoted the CFO's own LinkedIn post. They'd referenced a challenging initiative from the annual report. They'd used the exact language the executive team was using internally.

Tom hadn't lost because he didn't understand the business. He lost because he didn't do the homework that proved it.

The Turnaround

For his next pitch‚Äîsimilar client profile, similar stakeholder‚ÄîTom spent 20 minutes with AI analysing recent annual reports, executive LinkedIn posts (last 6 months), and earnings calls.

The Discovery

The CFO mentioned "cost-to-serve" 17 times across those documents.

"Innovation" appeared twice.

The annual report buried a failed automation project in the risk disclosures section.

The Repositioning

Tom didn't pitch "digital transformation"‚Äîwhat everyone else was selling. He led with "operational efficiency with managed risk."

He referenced their past experience directly: "I know change management is challenging. Here's how we de-risk this while delivering the efficiency gains you need."

Outcome

Won. Significant multi-year contract.

The competitive edge? Not "better AI skills." Better pattern recognition applied to better data. Tom knew what "cost-to-serve mentioned 17 times" meant. AI just surfaced it faster.

Timme & Astley ‚Äî Insight-Led Selling

Tom's approach is textbook Insight-Led Selling: Adopt an Executive Mindset, Build Credibility, Communicate with Impact.

Adopt an Executive Mindset: Tom shifted from "what am I selling?" to "what are THEY trying to solve?" He started with the CFO's priorities‚Äîcost-to-serve optimisation, operational efficiency, risk management.

Build Credibility: By referencing their past automation challenges and acknowledging change management complexity, Tom proved he'd done the homework AND understood the context.

Communicate with Impact: Tom used THEIR language. "Cost-to-serve." "Operational efficiency." "Managed risk." He didn't translate their priorities into vendor jargon.

Chris Voss ‚Äî Tactical Empathy

Tom's acknowledgment of their past automation challenges is pure Voss: "I know change management is challenging." This is tactical empathy‚Äîproving you understand their world before asking for anything. Voss calls this "accusation audit"‚Äîsurfacing the objection before they voice it.

McKinsey ‚Äî Second-Order Thinking

First-order thinking: They want innovation ‚Üí Pitch our latest AI capabilities.

Second-order thinking: They've been burned before ‚Üí They want efficiency without risk ‚Üí Position around proven delivery with managed change.

AI didn't give Tom this insight. His experience did. AI just surfaced the evidence faster.

The Framework

Goals ‚Üí Strategies ‚Üí Initiatives ‚Üí Departments

One of the most powerful ideas in Insight-Led Selling is this: companies don't buy products. They buy to implement their initiatives in support of their strategies and goals. Most sellers stop at the department level. Executives think in the hierarchy:

1

Goals

What they want to achieve‚Äîvision, financial targets, strategic imperatives

2

Strategies

3

4

Compliance & Data Protection Warning

Before using ANY AI tool with client, company, or deal data, read this carefully.

What's Safe to Upload: Published annual reports, press releases, public LinkedIn profiles, earnings call transcripts, news articles, publicly available conference presentations.

What's NOT Safe (Without Legal/IT Approval): CRM data, proposals with pricing, internal strategy documents, confidential customer information, regulated data (GDPR, financial services, healthcare), competitive intelligence marked confidential, contract terms.

The Rule: If you wouldn't post it publicly on LinkedIn, don't upload it to a free AI tool.

Free AI tools may use your inputs for training unless you're on enterprise plans with data protection agreements. Before using Level 3 or beyond: check your company's AI usage policy, verify data classification, anonymize where possible, use enterprise AI tools if available, and when in doubt, ask Legal/IT/Compliance.

Honesty Check

When AI Gets It Wrong

We flagged two of these earlier. Here's the full picture‚Äîthree failure modes, and how to handle each:

1

The Hallucination Problem

AI synthesised "strategic priorities" that sounded credible but weren't in the source documents. Nearly cited a fake initiative in a pitch.

‚Üí Fix: Ask AI to cite page numbers or quotes. If it can't, don't use it.

2

The Over-Reliance Trap

Used AI-generated questions without adding personal judgment. Questions were generic. Client said "everyone asks those questions."

‚Üí Fix: AI gives the starting point. Your experience adds the depth.

3

The Context Collapse

AI combined information from different years as if current. Referenced an executive who'd left 18 months prior.

‚Üí Fix: Always verify dates and current roles. LinkedIn is your check.
