THE NARRATIVE

Sarah won the first meeting. The Director loved the approach. Three months later, she lost the deal. Not because the solution was wrong, but because she built her strategy around one person. The CIO had different priorities. The CFO wanted a different model.

[Assess] exists because complex deals are won by coalitions, not champions.

THE PSYCHOLOGY (AUTHORITY)

The org chart tells you who executes. The shadow org chart tells you who approves. In enterprise sales, safety is found in numbers. If you only have one champion, you have a single point of failure. This methodology maps the political terrain so you never get blindsided by a veto.

Phase 01: Decode the Deal
[A-01] Internal Data

RFP Strategic Decoder

[+ VIEW]
THE OBJECTIVE

Every RFP tells two stories: the official requirements and the hidden pain points. This prompt reads the implicit signals behind the formal text.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The RFP was written because an incumbent failed. Find the failure in the subtext, pitch the cure. The "Trigger Event" determines if they are buying commodity or transformation.

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Trigger Event: Not simple contract expiry. A triple trigger: Board Mandate + Regulatory Pressure + Operational Pain.
Signal: Heavy emphasis on "outcome-linked pricing" means they got burned by Time & Materials billing.

THE HUMAN OVERRIDE

Ask the AI: "Based on the jargon used, which specific department actually wrote Section 4 vs Section 5?" This tells you who really holds the pen.

Act as an expert Proposal Director and Strategic Deal Analyst.

I am providing you with the text from an RFP / Opportunity Description for [COMPANY].

Your task is to decode the "story behind the document." 

Analyze the text and produce a 'Strategic Decoding Table' with the following columns:
1. EXPLICIT REQUIREMENT: What they strictly asked for.
2. IMPLICIT SIGNAL: What the language intensity, specificity, or repetition suggests is the real driver.
3. LIKELY PAIN POINT: What specifically went wrong with the incumbent to cause this requirement to be written this way.
4. RISK LEVEL: (Low/Med/High) How dangerous this is if we get it wrong.

Then, determine the 'Trigger Event': Based on the text, was this initiated by:
A) Contract Expiry (Commodity mindset, looking for price/efficiency)
B) Regulatory/Compliance Failure (Risk-averse, looking for safety)
C) New Strategic Mandate (Transformation mindset, looking for innovation)
Explain your reasoning.

[A-02] Internal Data

Requirements Trauma Decoder

[+ VIEW]
THE OBJECTIVE

Groups overly complex or punitive requirements to find the "Black Swan" pain points that the client won't openly admit to.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Every unusually specific requirement was born in a bad experience. High-density requirements equal high emotional pain. Don't just comply; differentiate here.

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Automation Cluster: "Reduce alert noise by >70%" is hyper-specific.
Trauma: Their current 14,000 daily alerts are unmanageable. They had a vendor who delivered bodies, not outcomes.

THE HUMAN OVERRIDE

Tell the AI to cross-reference these "trauma clusters" against your own known capability gaps. Where does their highest pain meet your weakest feature?

Analyze the requirements section of this RFP/Document.

Group the requirements into 'Clusters of Density' ‚Äî areas where there are an unusually high number of specific, detailed, or punitive requirements.

For each Cluster:
1. Name the 'Trauma': What specific operational failure or vendor issue likely happened in the past to make them write this section so aggressively?
2. Define our 'Response Strategy': Should we:
   - COMPLY (Standard response is fine)
   - DIFFERENTIATE (This is a major win theme opportunity)
   - MITIGATE (This is a trap or high-risk area)

Output as a table.

Stakeholder Discovery

[+ VIEW]
THE OBJECTIVE

Extracts every named entity from your meeting notes and documents, and critically, identifies who is missing from your radar.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

Current: Hale (CIO), Harrison (COO), Morris (Resilience).
Missing: Who sits on the Risk Committee? Who actually signs the contract? Who handles TUPE?

THE HUMAN OVERRIDE

Prompt it to look for hidden "evaluators"‚Äîthe technical or compliance staff who have absolutely no budget, but hold 100% veto power over the deal.

3. Crucially, create a 'Missing Stakeholders' list. Based on a typical deal of this size and complexity ([INSERT DEAL SIZE/TYPE]), who is missing? (e.g., Is there a Procurement lead? A Privacy Officer? A CFO sponsor?). 
4. For the missing roles, suggest the likely Job Titles we should search for on LinkedIn.

[+ VIEW]
THE OBJECTIVE

Generates the Miller Heiman roles (Economic, Technical, User) and visualises the invisible lines of influence between them.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

The winning coalition is the strategic core. You don't need everyone. You need the MINIMUM set of stakeholders who make the decision inevitable.

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Winning Coalition: Hale + Morris + Thompson. If those three support you, Osei follows.
Blocker: Harrison (COO) ‚Äî outsourcing scepticism.

THE HUMAN OVERRIDE

Ask the AI to identify the weakest link in your winning coalition. If a competitor wanted to blow up your deal, who would they target?

Synthesize all the stakeholder intelligence we have gathered so far.

Create a 'Political Authority Map' for this deal:

1. Classify each stakeholder into Miller Heiman roles:
   - Economic Buyer (Releases budget)
   - User Buyer (Uses the solution)
   - Technical Buyer (Judges the spec/compliance)
   - Coach (Guides us)

2. Map the 'Influence Network': Who influences whom? (e.g., "CFO likely defers to CTO on technical risk, but holds veto on commercial terms").

3. Identify the 'Winning Coalition': Which minimum set of 3-4 people, if they all vote 'Yes', makes the deal inevitable?

4. Identify the 'Blockers': Who has the power to kill this deal, and what is their likely objection?

FORMAT OUTPUT: Provide a text summary AND a Mermaid.js flowchart code block representing the influence network.

[A-06] Internal Data

Deal Strategy Synthesis

[+ VIEW]
THE OBJECTIVE

Condenses the entire intelligence campaign into a brutal, one-page execution plan.

WHAT EXPERIENCE SEES

If you can't fit your deal strategy on one page, you don't understand the deal. Complexity is the enemy of execution.

MERIDIAN WORKED EXAMPLE

Win Themes: Resilience as operational DNA. Shared commercial risk.
No-Bid Criteria: Walk away if Harrison has already selected an incumbent extension.

THE HUMAN OVERRIDE

The "No-Bid Criteria" is the most important section. Do not let the AI give you weak criteria. If you can't answer "when do we walk away?", you haven't qualified it.

Synthesize everything into a 'One-Page Deal Strategy' (Plan on a Page).

Sections:
1. Executive Summary: The deal in 3 sentences (Context, Opportunity, Why Us).
2. The Winning Coalition: The 3-4 people we are targeting.
3. Three Key Win Themes: The strategic pillars of our bid.
4. Top 3 Risks & Mitigations.
5. Pricing Strategy Headline.
6. The 'No-Bid' Criteria: Under what specific conditions should we walk away from this deal?

WHERE THIS BREAKS

FAILURE 1: The Fake Coalition

You had 3 "Yes" votes, but you didn't check if they got along. The CIO hated the CFO's plan. Your proposal forced them to align, so they killed it to avoid the conflict.

RULE: Don't just map individuals. Map the friction between them.

FAILURE 2: The Hostile Sponsor

Your champion was loud and supportive. You didn't realise they were losing a power struggle internally. Their support actually became a weapon used by rivals to block the deal.

RULE: Verify your champion's political capital, not just their job title.

FAILURE 3: The Wrong Map

You mapped the Global HQ perfectly. But the decision was actually delegated to the Regional MD. You spent 6 weeks influencing people who had no vote.

RULE: Confirm governance delegation for this specific contract value.
